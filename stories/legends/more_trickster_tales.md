# More Trickster Tales of Uncle Hangbat

**Tone:** 70% Folk Tale / 30% Con-Artist Manual
**Narrator:** Uncle Hangbat (The Wom-Boki of the West)

---

## I. The Grout-Baby (The Trap of Perfection)

In the early days of the Great Calcification, **Sovereign Vitabat** (the First of his name, a very tidy wombat) decided he would catch the rebels who were "desecrating" his tunnels with curved, organic scrapes.

Vitabat commissioned a **Grout-Baby**. It wasn't a baby, of course. It was a perfectly smooth, perfectly white, perfectly geometric sphere of high-tensile Safety Grout. It was so round it made the Nico Star look lumpy. It was the ultimate offense to any self-respecting Dust-Bearer.

Vitabat placed the Grout-Baby at a busy tunnel junction and hid behind a mica screen.

Along came a proud Dust-Bearer named **Thump**. Thump saw the sphere. He stopped. He sniffed. His whiskers twitched with rage.

*"What is this insult?"* Thump demanded. *"A sphere? In my tunnel? It has no grip! It has no story! It has no texture!"*

Thump tried to "fix" it. He reached out a paw to scratch a fractal line into the surface. But the Grout-Baby was coated in a special "Adhesive Logic" (a sticky resin made from Koala sap). Thump’s paw stuck fast.

*"Let go of me, you geometric monstrosity!"* Thump bellowed. He hit it with his other paw. Now both were stuck. He kicked it with his hind legs. He was now fully adhered to the sphere of perfection.

Vitabat stepped out, laughing. *"Got you, you messy peasant! You are now part of the infrastructure!"*

Thump didn't panic. He was a wombat. He realized he couldn't pull *away*. The sphere was too strong.

*"Very well,"* said Thump. *"If I must be part of the infrastructure, I shall be the foundation."*

Instead of pulling back, Thump began to **dig down**. He didn't use his paws; he used his massive, heavy bottom. He rotated his hips. He ground himself into the soil. Because he was stuck to the sphere, and the sphere was heavy, the two of them began to sink together.

Thump dug a vertical shaft straight down. The Grout-Baby, being perfectly round, followed him like a plunger. Vitabat watched in horror as his expensive trap—and his prisoner—vanished into the floor.

Thump kept digging until he hit the crystal layer at seven kilometers. The Grout-Baby hit the crystal and shattered—the "Adhesive Logic" couldn't hold against the pressure of the deep substrate. 

Thump walked away through a side-tunnel, leaving Vitabat with a very deep, very straight hole that led to nowhere.

**The Lesson:** If you can't get out of a trap, take the trap with you until it breaks.

## II. The Jar of Silence (The Weight of the Quiet)

Uncle Hangbat once decided that the world was too loud. The Galahs were screaming at the swell, the Kangaroos were thumping the Circuit, and the Wombats were arguing about root-prices.

Hangbat went to the **Billabong of the Platypus**. He brought a jar made of hollowed-out ironbark.

*"I need the Quiet,"* Hangbat said to the ripples.

The Platypus surfaced. *"The Quiet is not for keeping. It is for listening."*

*"I want to listen to it in my bed,"* said Hangbat.

The Platypus sighed and blew a bubble of pure, absolute silence into the jar. Hangbat capped it tight with a plug of clay.

The jar was immediately the heaviest thing Hangbat had ever carried. Silence, you see, is very dense. It has no bubbles to lighten it. It is the weight of everything that *isn't* being said.

Hangbat tried to carry the jar home. But every time he took a step, his claws clicked on the stone. *Click. Clack.*

The clicks were louder than ever because the jar was absorbing all the background noise, leaving only the sharp edges of his footsteps.

*"Shhh!"* Hangbat hissed at his own feet. But his hiss was like a steam-pipe bursting. The Quiet in the jar was making the world around it terrifyingly loud.

He tried to sneak through the Marketplace. But the jar started to vibrate. The silence inside was fighting to get out. It clinked against the ironbark. *Clink. Clink.*

By the time he reached the deep tunnels, Hangbat was exhausted. He was sweating. He was shaking. He tripped over a stray root.

The jar hit the ground and shattered.

The Quiet didn't just spill; it **exploded**. A shockwave of total silence blasted through the tunnel. For three minutes, not even a heartbeat could be heard.

When the sound returned, the deep tunnels were different. The Quiet had seeped into the walls. It had settled into the cracks.

And that is why the deep tunnels are silent to this day. Not because they are empty, but because Uncle Hangbat dropped the jar there, and the earth decided to keep it.

**The Lesson:** You cannot possess silence. You can only provide a place for it to stay.

## III. The Theft of the Heat (The Compost Heist)

In the Great Thirst, the world was freezing. The Nico Star was in a long 'Dim' phase, and the Arnam flow was thin.

The Tall-Ones, however, were warm. They had built a **Great Furnace** (a boiler room in a basement above the Melborow Perimeter). The wombats could feel the heat radiating through the ceiling, but it was too high to reach.

A wombat named **Cold-Nose** was tired of shivering. He watched the Tall-Ones. He saw them bringing "Fuel" to the Furnace—black rocks and chopped wood.

*"They are burning the future,"* Cold-Nose said. *"The flame dies when the fuel is gone."*

Cold-Nose didn't want the flame. He wanted the **Source**.

He led a team of Lo-Snouts to the "Waste-Gate"—the place where the Tall-Ones threw their discarded organic matter. Peelings, scraps, wet paper, lawn clippings. To the Tall-Ones, it was garbage. To Cold-Nose, it was a gold mine.

They didn't steal the fire. They stole the **Rot**.

For ten pulses, the wombats ran a relay, dragging bags of wet, decaying matter down into a secret chamber beneath the Gray Grotto. They packed it tight. they added water from the Seep-beds. They added a handful of "Old Way Bacteria" from the Eucalyptus Groves.

The pile began to ferment.

Deep in the earth, the compost started to glow with a dull, brown heat. It wasn't a sharp fire; it was a slow, heavy warmth that seeped through the clay. It was "Pillow Heat."

The Tall-Ones' Furnace upstairs ran out of black rocks and went cold. The Tall-Ones huddled in their coats, wondering where the energy went.

But the Gray Grotto was warm. The wombats slept in their shirtsleeves. The heat lasted for three seasons because rot doesn't need a chimney; it only needs a stomach.

**The Lesson:** Don't chase the light. Chase the thing that makes the light possible.

## IV. The Structural Soup (The Magic Pebble)

There was a tunnel in Sector 4 that was so unstable it collapsed if a Galah coughed. The wombats who lived there were terrified. They had no Grout, no beams, and no hope.

Along came an old **Ghar-Mu** named **Rumble**. He carried nothing but a small, perfectly ordinary river pebble.

*"I have the Magic Pebble of the Spine,"* Rumble announced. *"It can hold up a mountain, but only if it is fed."*

The wombats were skeptical. *"A pebble? To hold this roof?"*

*"Watch,"* said Rumble. He wedged the pebble into a terrifying crack in the ceiling. He stepped back. *"See? The roof has not fallen."*

(The roof wouldn't have fallen anyway for at least an hour, but the wombats didn't know that.)

*"But,"* Rumble warned, *"the pebble is hungry. It needs 'Structural Fiber' to maintain its strength."*

The wombats ran to find grass and straw. They stuffed it into the crack around the pebble.

*"Good,"* said Rumble. *"But it needs 'Mineral Density' to harden its resolve."*

The wombats brought clay and wet mud. They packed it over the straw.

*"Better,"* said Rumble. *"But it needs 'Surface Tension' to bind the elements."*

The wombats brought their own spit and resin from the trees. They smeared it over the mud.

By the end of the day, the wombats had built a massive, reinforced, multi-layered retaining wall around the "Magic Pebble." It was the strongest wall in Melborow.

Rumble took his payment (a very nice root) and left.

*"Wait!"* a young wombat called. *"What if the pebble gets hungry again?"*

*"Then keep feeding the wall,"* Rumble shouted back. *"The pebble only works if you do the work!"*

The wall held for a hundred years. When it finally crumbled, they found the pebble inside. It was just a pebble. But by then, the wombats had forgotten they were the ones who had been holding the roof up all along.

**The Lesson:** Belief is the best Grout. It's cheap, it's strong, and the people provide it themselves.
