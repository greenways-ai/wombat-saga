# The Age of the Primes

**Tone:** 80% Mythic-Wry (Rowling) / 20% Structural-Physics (Asimov)
**Time:** Before the Great Disconnection. Before the Static. Before anything went wrong.

---

## Part One: The Kangaroo — The Vita Patrolum

### I. The Circuit Was the Law

In the old days, the Kangaroos did not have courts. They did not have councils. They did not have meetings where someone stood at the front and talked until everyone else lost the will to disagree, which is the wombat method and always has been.

The Kangaroos had the Circuit.

Every mob ran a territorial loop — a vast, elliptical patrol that could take three full pulses of Elen-Nico to complete. The dominant doe set the route. The boomers followed. The joeys bounced in the pouches, learning the geography of governance before they could walk.

The Circuit was not a fence. It was a *conversation*. Every impact — every landing of those enormous hind feet, every percussive thud of a tail on soil — sent a seismic message through the substrate. The message said: *I am here. This ground is tended. The boundary holds.* The topsoil compacted beneath them in a specific pattern — organic, fractal, irregular enough to resist the lattice but firm enough to carry weight. They were paving the world with their bodies, and the pavement was alive.

Creatures who lived inside a Circuit lived under the Kangaroo's peace. Not because anyone said so. Because the ground told them. You could feel it through your paws, your claws, your belly — the deep, rhythmic vibration of a mob in motion, and the vibration meant *order*, and order meant *safety*, and safety meant you could get on with the business of being whatever you were without worrying about what was coming over the hill.

The Emus called the Kangaroo patrols "The Drum Beneath the Drum" — the beat under the world's heartbeat, the rhythm beneath Elen-Nico's pulse. They respected it the way warriors respect an older weapon.

### II. The Dispute at Dry Channels

The story the old birds tell most often — the one that shows how the Kangaroos *worked* — is the Dispute at Dry Channels.

Two dingo clans had been running the same relay through the western shell for three generations. The northern clan carried messages from the Salt Pan to the Gorge. The southern clan carried messages from the Gorge to the Emu Steppe. The relay point was a flat rock at the junction of two dry riverbeds — the Channels — and for three generations, the two clans had shared it without incident, because the rock was big enough for both and the messages were different and there was no reason to fight.

Then the riverbeds shifted. The northern channel silted up. The southern channel deepened. The flat rock — the relay point, the junction, the place where messages changed hands — was now on the southern bank, which meant it was now in the southern clan's territory, which meant the northern clan had to cross a territorial line to reach it, which meant asking permission, which dingoes do not do. Dingoes do not ask permission the way galahs do not whisper: the concept exists in theory but has no practical application.

So the northern clan crossed without asking. The southern clan bared teeth. Within two pulses, there were forty dingoes on a dry riverbed doing the thing that dingoes do when they disagree, which is standing very still and vibrating with the promise of violence.

The Kangaroos arrived on the third pulse.

Not one mob. Three. Converging from three directions, thirty animals in each, running their Circuits in a pattern that — if you'd been watching from above, as the Galahs were — formed a triangle around the disputed riverbed. They didn't stop. They didn't speak. They simply ran the Circuit, and the Circuit enclosed the dispute, and the seismic message of ninety kangaroos hitting the ground in a coordinated pattern said, very clearly, to every creature within ten kilometres: *stand still*.

The dingoes stood still. Not because they were afraid. Because the ground under their paws was telling them to, and a dingo who ignores the ground is a dingo who has forgotten what his feet are for.

Then Mountbatten — the High-Peak, the dominant boomer of the eastern mob — did the thing.

He stopped in the middle of the triangle. He looked at the northern dingoes. He looked at the southern dingoes. He looked at the flat rock.

And he thumped his tail.

Once. A single, vertical percussion that hit the soil and radiated outward through the substrate like a stone dropped into still water. The thump carried data: the density of the ground, the depth of the water table, the stress patterns in the riverbed, the silt that had shifted the channel. It was a seismic survey compressed into one beat. And every creature in range — every dingo, every kangaroo, every galah watching from the ridge — felt it in their bones.

The thump said: the riverbed will shift again. The northern channel will clear in two seasons. The rock will be on neither bank. The dispute is about a geography that doesn't exist yet.

Mountbatten hopped away. The three mobs closed their triangular Circuit and continued their patrol.

The dingoes looked at each other. Looked at the ground. Felt the residual vibration of the thump — the data still settling through the substrate, the truth of it ringing in the soil the way a bell rings after the hand has left it.

They shared the rock.

Not because a Kangaroo told them to. Because the ground told them the argument was temporary, and a dingo who fights over temporary ground is a dingo who has mistaken geography for identity.

### III. The Friction Engine

The Kangaroos called it the Bunda — the Will. The Great Leap. Not just the physical act of hopping but the philosophical engine that drove it: the conversion of resistance into motion, of opposition into energy, of friction into function.

When a dispute was small — a wallaby mob arguing over a feeding ground, a wombat clan squabbling about tunnel rights — a single Kangaroo circuit was enough. The mob would run the boundary of the disputed area, and the vibration of their passage would compact the soil into a neutral strip — a no-creature's-land that belonged to the patrol and therefore to no one and therefore to everyone. The strip wasn't a wall. It was a *margin*. A space where the argument couldn't take root because the ground was already spoken for.

When a dispute was large — when whole regions threatened to fracture, when the Koalas' pheromone regulation wasn't enough, when the Platypus had spoken in riddles and nobody listened — the Kangaroos escalated.

They called it the Convergence.

Every mob in Hún Nico would run its Circuit simultaneously. Not their usual ellipses — a single, collective loop, spiralling inward from the widest point of the Crown toward the centre, hundreds of kangaroos pounding the inner surface of the world in a shrinking gyre, the seismic energy building with each revolution, the substrate vibrating at a frequency so deep it was felt in the crystal layer seven kilometres down.

The Convergence did not stop disputes. It *grounded* them. The kinetic energy of all that coordinated impact dissipated the tension the way an earthing wire dissipates lightning — taking the charge, converting it to heat, letting it bleed into the substrate where it could do no harm.

After a Convergence, the world was quiet. Not Koala-quiet — the deliberate, maintained silence of a creature holding the void. Kangaroo-quiet. The quiet that comes after something has been thoroughly *hit*. The quiet of a nail that's been driven home.

The old Galahs say a Convergence happened exactly four times in the history of Hún Nico. The last one was three generations before the Static began to rise. After that, the Circuits started fragmenting — mobs breaking away, running smaller loops, turning the spiral inward instead of outward, converting the Friction Engine from a machine that produced stability into a machine that produced violence.

The engine still turns. It just doesn't drive anything.

## Part Two: The Koala — The Alchemical Silence

### I. The Quiet

The first thing a Koala learned — before climbing, before chewing, before the long art of sleeping twenty hours a day in a posture that looked boneless but was actually a precisely calibrated thermal regulation position — was the Quiet.

Not silence. Silence is the absence of sound. The Quiet is something else entirely.

The Quiet is the background hum of the world against which all other sounds are measured. It is the zero line. The baseline. The frequency that exists when nothing else is happening, and it is not nothing — it is the sound of Hún Nico being alive. The sound of substrate settling. The sound of crystal humming at the seven-kilometre depth. The sound of roots drinking. The sound of the Glass — five centimetres of mirrored boundary — vibrating at a frequency so low it registers not as sound but as *certainty*.

Without the Quiet, you cannot hear. Not because your ears stop working but because hearing requires a reference point. You hear a bird call because the bird call is louder than the Quiet. You hear a footstep because the footstep disrupts the Quiet. Remove the Quiet and every sound arrives at the same volume, the same urgency, the same weight, and the result is not cacophony — it's *blindness*. Acoustic blindness. The inability to tell what matters from what doesn't.

The Koalas held the Quiet the way the Kangaroos held the ground: not by enforcing it, but by *being* it. A Koala sitting in a fork of a Eucalyptus, apparently asleep, was not asleep. She was *listening* — reading the thermal gradients of the air, tasting the chemical signature of the breeze, calibrating her own metabolic frequency to match the baseline hum of the world. She was a living tuning fork, and her body was the instrument that told every other creature: *this is the zero. This is what normal sounds like. Everything else is signal.*

### II. The Drop

When things went wrong — when a dispute heated past the point where the Kangaroo Circuits could cool it, when the chemical balance of a region tipped from healthy friction into toxic volatility — the Koalas performed the Drop.

It began with the Elemental Scan. The senior Koala — always a female, because the females held the deeper thermal register — would open her metabolic channels fully, reading the region the way a Galah reads an echo-return: not with one sense but with all of them. Temperature. Humidity. Pheromone density. The chemical markers of fear, aggression, confusion, grief. Each emotion had a colour — the old Koalas called them the Four Humors, and in the Golden Wattle Dreams, when the pollen hung heavy and the trance came down, the Humors were *visible*. Fire-red for rage. Wind-white for panic. Water-blue for sorrow. Earth-brown for stubbornness.

A skilled Koala could see the Humors in a creature the way you or I see the colour of a flower. She'd look at a dingo and see the red threading through his fur. She'd look at a wombat and see the brown settling in his shoulders like silt in a slow river.

When the Scan was complete, the Koala knew the chemistry of the crisis. And then she dropped.

Not fell. *Dropped.* A deliberate descent from the high canopy — from the branches nearest Elen-Nico's warmth, where the air was thin and clean — down to the ground. Down to where the creatures were. Down into the heat of it.

The Drop was metabolic sacrifice. The Koala's body, calibrated for the canopy's cool, hit the ground-level chemistry like a cold stone dropped into hot water. She absorbed it. The pheromones of forty angry dingoes, the cortisol of a wombat clan in panic, the sharp electric tang of a Kangaroo mob running too hot — all of it flooded into her system, and her body did what Koala bodies were built to do: *transmute*.

The Menthol. That's what the old stories call it. The Koala breathed in fire and breathed out frost. Not literally — or not only literally. The exhalation carried a chemical compound that the Koalas produced in their secondary stomachs, derived from the Eucalyptus oils they spent twenty hours a day processing. The compound was volatile, airborne, fast-acting. It reached the limbic systems of every warm-blooded creature within a hundred metres and said, in the language of chemistry: *the temperature is dropping. You are safe. Stand down.*

It wasn't mind control. It wasn't sedation. It was *context*. The Koala was resetting the chemical environment to baseline, the way a tuning fork resets the pitch. The anger was still there. The dispute was still there. But the chemistry that had been making the anger *feel* like the only option evaporated, and the creatures found themselves standing in the aftermath of an almost-fight, wondering why their muscles were unclenching and their fur was lying flat.

The Koala, for her part, looked like she'd run a marathon. The Drop cost everything. She'd climb back to the canopy shaking, over-warm, chemically depleted, and sleep for two days. The other Koalas would monitor her, adjusting the canopy's thermal balance to support her recovery, feeding her specific leaf-concentrations to replenish the compounds she'd burned.

It was medicine. The Koala was the pharmacist and the pharmacy and the dose. And the dose, as every Koala knew, makes the poison.

### III. The Diagnosis of the Borow War

The story the Koalas tell — when they were still telling stories, before the Lyptus turned memory into merchandise — is the Diagnosis of the Borow War.

Three wombat clans in the mid-latitudes had been disputing a tunnel junction for eight pulses. The junction connected three deep-dig networks, and each clan claimed the right to maintain it — which, for wombats, was the same as claiming the right to exist, because a wombat who doesn't dig is a wombat who has forgotten his own name.

The Kangaroos had run Circuits around the surface entrances. The dingoes had carried messages between the clans. The Galahs had screamed about it extensively, which didn't help but made the Galahs feel better, which was the only outcome Galahs ever expected from screaming.

Nothing worked. The dispute was underground, and the Kangaroo's seismic thumps couldn't reach a junction that was four kilometres deep. The chemistry was too buried, too slow, too wombat. Wombat disputes don't burn hot the way dingo disputes do. They smoulder. They sit. They accumulate pressure the way substrate accumulates crystal — incrementally, invisibly, and then one day the ceiling collapses.

So old Eucal — the last of the true Smith-Koalas, the sleep-mother of the northern canopy — did a thing that hadn't been done in living memory. She went *underground*.

A Koala underground. Think about that. A creature calibrated for the canopy — for thin air, for the high thermal register, for the light of Elen-Nico filtered through leaf-cover — voluntarily entering a wombat tunnel. It was like asking a fish to review a desert.

Eucal went anyway. She descended through the eastern shaft — slowly, carefully, her claws gripping the tunnel walls in a way that left marks the wombats would find puzzling for generations, because Koala claws leave a scratch-pattern that spirals, and wombat tunnels don't spiral, and the dissonance between the two was the first sign that something unprecedented was happening.

She reached the junction. The three clans were there — thirty wombats, shoulder to shoulder, in a space designed for ten. The air was thick with the pheromones of wombat stubbornness, which, if you've never smelled it, is like wet clay mixed with absolute conviction.

Eucal sat in the junction. She closed her eyes. She ran the Elemental Scan — and what she read terrified her.

The wombats weren't fighting over the junction. The junction was the *symptom*. Three kilometres below the disputed tunnel, the crystal substrate was shifting. A hairline fracture in the lattice was redirecting the water table, and the three clans — without knowing it, without having the language for it — were arguing about water that was no longer there. Their instincts had registered the hydrological shift before their minds had. The dispute was their bodies trying to tell them something their culture couldn't express: *the ground has changed, and if we don't change with it, we'll dig into dead rock and suffocate.*

Eucal opened her eyes. She looked at each clan leader. She said — and this is the bit the Koalas always quote, the bit that makes the young ones go quiet:

"You are not fighting each other. You are fighting the ground. And the ground is trying to help you."

Then she told them where the water had gone.

The three clans dug a new junction — together, because the new route was too deep for any single clan — and the dispute dissolved like Menthol in warm air. Not resolved. *Dissolved.* The Koala hadn't brokered a compromise. She'd diagnosed the disease that the argument was a symptom of, and once the disease was named, the symptom stopped mattering.

That was the Koala way. Not mediation. *Diagnosis.* The world doesn't have disputes. It has imbalances. Name the imbalance and the dispute treats itself.

## Part Three: The Platypus — The Paradox

### I. The Billabong

The Platypus lived in the boundary.

Not at the boundary. Not near the boundary. *In* the boundary — in the liminal space between water and earth, between swimming and walking, between the thing a creature is and the thing it isn't. The billabongs were their domain: those still, brown pools where the soil became liquid and the water became solid and the difference between the two was a question that the Platypus refused to answer, not out of evasion but out of principle.

"The boundary between solid and fluid is a lie," old Migo used to say. Then he'd sink into the mud to prove it, and the mud would close over his bill, and for a moment you couldn't tell where the Platypus ended and the billabong began, which was the point.

The Platypus were not mediators. The Kangaroos mediated — through force, through the ground, through the seismic grammar of the Circuit. The Koalas mediated — through chemistry, through the metabolic sacrifice of the Drop, through diagnosis. The Platypus did something stranger.

They *dissolved the premise*.

### II. The Electro-Sense

Every Platypus was born with the bill. Not just a beak — a sensory organ of such precision that it could read the electrical field of a living creature from fifty metres away. Every heartbeat generates an electrical signal. Every muscular contraction. Every neural impulse. The bill read them all, and the reading was not approximate. It was forensic.

A Platypus could not be lied to.

This was not a talent or a skill. It was a structural fact. The bill read the electrical signature of intent — not what a creature said, not what it meant to say, not the story it was telling itself about its own motivations, but the raw, pre-verbal, pre-conscious *signal* that fired in the neural tissue before the thought formed. The Platypus saw the thought before the thinker had it.

In the time of the Primes, this was the Unshakeable Gaze — the ability to see the source code behind the user interface. The Platypus looked at a dispute and saw not the argument but the architecture of the argument: who was afraid, who was pretending, who believed their own lie, who knew the truth but couldn't afford to say it.

And then — this was the part that made other creatures deeply uncomfortable — the Platypus would tell you what it saw. Not bluntly. Not cruelly. In riddles.

### III. The Riddle of the Three Burrows

The story the old Dust-Bearers whisper — and they whisper it, because it concerns a Platypus, and Platypus stories are always whispered, as though speaking them aloud might summon the creature back from wherever it went — is the Riddle of the Three Burrows.

A young wombat named Hoegrund had dug three burrows on the eastern slope. Not because he needed three. One was enough. But Hoegrund was the kind of wombat who digs the way some creatures breathe — compulsively, excessively, because the alternative is standing still and a wombat who stands still starts thinking, and thinking, for a wombat, leads to uncomfortable places.

The first burrow was deep and dry and structurally sound. A perfect burrow. The kind the Root-Counters would hold up as an example.

The second burrow was shallow and wide and opened onto a view of the Dry Channels, which is beautiful and entirely impractical for a burrow, because a burrow that opens onto a view is a burrow that lets the weather in, and weather is what burrows exist to avoid.

The third burrow was unfinished. Half-dug. Abandoned at the point where the soil changed — where the loose loam met the harder substrate and the digging got difficult. Hoegrund had reached the resistance and stopped.

Hoegrund came to the billabong — not because he wanted advice, he'd say later, but because his paws hurt and the water was cool and a wombat is allowed to soak his paws without it meaning anything. But the Platypus was there. Of course the Platypus was there. The Platypus was always there, in the way that the boundary between water and mud is always there — you just forget it exists until you step in it.

"Which burrow should I live in?" Hoegrund asked, because wombats, when they accidentally find themselves in the presence of a Platypus, tend to blurt out the question they've been carrying without meaning to.

The Platypus — later identified as Gopa, the heat-reader, the one whose bill could taste the thermal gradient of a creature's doubt — sank until only her eyes were above the waterline. She looked at Hoegrund. She looked at him for a long time. Long enough that Hoegrund began to regret having paws, and feet, and a body that was standing at the edge of a billabong when it could have been underground not having this conversation.

"The first burrow is the one you built for your father," Gopa said. "The second is the one you built for yourself. The third is the one you built for the ground."

And she sank below the surface and didn't come back up.

Hoegrund stood there for a long time. Then he went back to the third burrow — the unfinished one, the one he'd abandoned when the digging got hard — and he kept digging. Past the resistance. Past the substrate change. Into the harder, denser earth that fought back and demanded technique and patience and the willingness to work with what the ground wanted instead of what the wombat wanted.

He hit water at seven metres. A seep — a tiny, persistent trickle that came from a vein in the substrate that no one had mapped. The water was clean. The water was old. The water connected, through hairline channels in the rock, to the deep aquifer that the Platypus had been maintaining since the Age of Chaos.

Hoegrund's third burrow became the seed of what would later be called the Seep-Beds — *Néncaima* — the deep-level water access points that the Dust-Bearers would maintain for generations. A network of tunnels built not where the wombat wanted to dig, but where the ground wanted to be opened.

All because a Platypus told him something he already knew but couldn't say to himself: the first burrow was duty. The second was desire. The third was the one that mattered, because the third was the one where he stopped performing and started *listening*.

### IV. The Paradox Court

When the disputes were too large for riddles — when whole species were involved, when the chemical and the seismic had failed — the Platypus convened the Paradox Court.

It wasn't a court in any sense that a wombat or a kangaroo would recognise. There was no judge. No testimony. No verdict. There was the billabong, and there was the Platypus, and there was the question.

The disputing parties stood at the edge. The Platypus floated in the middle. And the Platypus asked questions.

Not questions about the dispute. Questions about the *categories* that made the dispute possible.

When the dingoes and the kangaroos fought over the Serpent's Spine — the continuous ridge that coils around the entire surface of Hún Nico, splitting the eastern and western shells — the Platypus did not ask "who owns the Spine?" The Platypus asked: "Is the Spine one ridge or many?"

The dingoes said one. The Serpent's Spine is one continuous feature — they could prove it, they'd run the whole length, relay by relay, message by message.

The kangaroos said many. The Spine passes through different territories, different soils, different conditions — each section is unique, each section requires its own Circuit.

The Platypus said: "You are both right. It is one thing that is also many things. The question is not who owns it. The question is whether you believe a thing must be one or the other."

Then she dove. And when she surfaced, she was holding mud — not in her paws, not in her bill, but *on* her. The mud clung to her fur and her bill and her tail, and you could not tell where the Platypus ended and the mud began, and the mud was neither solid nor liquid, and the Platypus was neither swimming nor walking, and the boundary between was — had always been — a fiction.

The dingoes and the kangaroos looked at the mud-covered Platypus floating in the still brown water of the billabong. And they understood — not intellectually, not as a reasoned conclusion, but in the way you understand a joke: suddenly, completely, and slightly too late.

The Spine wasn't theirs to divide because the Spine wasn't divided. It was continuous. They had been fighting over how to cut a circle, which is a fight that has no answer because a circle has no edges.

The Paradox Court didn't resolve disputes. It *revealed the absurdity of the premise*. And once the premise was absurd, the dispute couldn't stand on it anymore, the way a building can't stand on a foundation that turns out to be water.

### V. The Vanishing

The Platypus existed in the space between categories. Fur and bill. Land and water. Venom and nurture. Mammal that lays eggs. Creature that shouldn't be possible but is.

When the Static began to rise — when the crystal lattice started creeping upward through the substrate, tiling the messy, organic, fractal world into geometric perfection — the Platypus were the first to feel it. Because the lattice is a system of categories. Hexagonal. Binary. This or that. Solid or fluid. Inside the line or outside it.

The Platypus could not exist in a world of categories.

Not "chose not to." *Could not.* The way a wave cannot exist in ice. The way a question cannot exist in an answer. The Platypus was the living embodiment of "both and neither," and the lattice was a structure that had no column for "both and neither." It had columns for "this" and columns for "that," and between them was nothing — not even a Compassion Gap. Just the clean, geometric edge where one hexagon met the next.

They didn't fight the Static. They didn't protest. They didn't even leave — not in any direction that the other creatures could follow.

They went *between*.

The billabongs dried. The mud hardened into geometry. The boundary between solid and fluid — the lie that the Platypus had spent an eternity exposing — became true, because the lattice made it true, and the lattice does not understand lies, and a world without lies has no room for paradox.

Old Us — the last of the Migopus Trio, the deep-root reader, the one who could hear the aquifer's pulse through three kilometres of substrate — was the last one seen. She stood at the edge of the last billabong, the water receding around her, the mud cracking into hexagons, and she said:

"We are not leaving. We are going to the place you stop looking."

Then the water closed, and the mud tiled, and the Platypus was gone.

And the world — which had been, until that moment, a place where things could be two things at once, where boundaries were negotiable, where a creature with fur could have a bill and a creature with a bill could have venom and the impossibility of it was the whole point — the world became a place where things were only one thing.

The Kangaroos felt it first. Without the Paradox to disrupt their categories, the Circuits became rigid. Territorial. Binary: inside or outside, us or them. The Friction Engine, which had been converting resistance into stability, began converting resistance into violence, because without paradox, resistance has only one meaning.

The Koalas felt it second. Without the Platypus maintaining the boundary between solid and fluid, the Quiet lost its reference point. The Elemental Scan still worked, but the diagnoses became simpler — Fire or Water, hot or cold, this Humor or that Humor. The subtle, between-state readings that had made the old Koalas great — the ability to see that a creature's anger was also its grief, that a clan's stubbornness was also its love — those readings required a world where things could be *both*. And both had left with the Platypus.

The Rosellas felt it last, and worst. The The Chorus was built on synthesis — on the ability to hold contradictory harmonics in tension until they resolved into a chord. Without the Paradox, synthesis collapsed into simplicity. Why hold the tension when the Lattice Song offers resolution? Why braid seven colours when one note rings clean?

Three Primes. Three gifts. Floor, Drink, Breath. The architecture of a world that worked because it was complicated, and messy, and full of creatures who shouldn't exist doing things that shouldn't be possible.

The Kangaroos are still here, running Circuits that have forgotten what they're for.

The Koalas are still here, selling silence that has forgotten what it's holding.

The Platypus is gone.

And the billabongs are hexagons now.
