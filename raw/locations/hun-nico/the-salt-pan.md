# The Salt Pan / *Palar Lossë*

*The white plain. Neutral territory. Where evaporated Arnam has left mineral crusts—white, cracked, blinding in the Star-light. Nothing grows. Nothing walks on it by choice. The Dingoes use it as a trading ground because unclaimed ground is the safest kind.*

---

## Physical Description

A vast flat basin in the south-west where the organic layer has surrendered entirely to mineral. The surface is white—not snow-white, not bone-white, but the white of salt crystals packed so densely they reflect the Nico Star in prismatic arcs that can blind the unprepared.

**The Crust:** Not solid. Cracked. A mosaic of irregular polygons, each plate slightly raised at the edges, creating a terrain of miniature cliffs and valleys. The cracks are where the Arnam has evaporated most completely, leaving pure mineral. The plates are where moisture still lingers, just below the surface, just enough to prevent total desiccation.

**The Flatness:** The Salt Pan is level in the way that only ancient lakebeds achieve. No feature rises more than a hand's height above the general surface. The horizon is a perfect line where white meets sky—a line that curves upward, eventually, because this is still the inner surface of the heart, but the curve is so gradual at this scale that it appears straight.

**The Silence:** Sound behaves strangely here. The salt absorbs high frequencies. Voices sound muffled, intimate, as if the air itself is thick. But low frequencies—footsteps, the shifting of crust plates—carry farther than they should, bouncing off the flat surface in ways that make distance difficult to judge.

---

## The Dingo Relay Stations

Dotting the perimeter, squat structures built from salt-blocks and Static-stone. The Dingoes have been here long enough to develop architecture from the only materials available.

**The Stations' Function:** Data transmission. The mineral crust conducts signal efficiently—salt is orderly; order carries data. The Dingoes have mapped the conductivity patterns, know which cracks transmit cleanly and which introduce noise. They communicate across the Pan in ways that the Council's Frequency cannot intercept.

**The Station Design:** Low, domed, partially buried. The salt-blocks are translucent—light filters through the walls in a diffuse white glow that makes day and night indistinguishable inside. The Dingoes who staff them rotate regularly. No one stays long enough to go mad from the sameness.

---

## The Trading Grounds

Specific locations on the Pan's edge where exchange happens. Not a market in the Melborow sense—no stalls, no currency, no Tally. The Dingoes' Static-Logic cannot accommodate those concepts. Instead: piles of goods, left in specific configurations, retrieved or added to by others.

**The Dingoes' Trade:** Static-ore harvested from the deep substrate. Lyptus-resin extracted from the Groves' trees. Information about Council movements. The occasional wombat seeking passage to the Lone Borrows without the Tally's notice.

**The Payment:** Filtered Frequency. Water access rights. Protection from the Kangaroo mobs who patrol the Burnt Scrub. The Dingoes do not give these things freely. They do not give anything freely. But they trade fairly, by their own logic, and their word—once given—is absolute.

## Sensory Texture

**Touch:** The salt crust is sharp at the edges, powdery on the surface. Walking on it requires care—the plates shift, crack, occasionally collapse into the hollows beneath. The mineral residue on your paws dries quickly, leaving a white film that itches. You do not touch your eyes after walking the Pan.

**Sound:** The muffled quality of voices. The hollow thud of footsteps on hollow plates. The occasional sharp crack of a plate breaking under weight or thermal stress. The wind, when it comes, produces a thin whistle through the cracks—a sound the Dingoes have learned to read for weather prediction.

**Smell:** Salt. Nothing but salt. The absence of organic decay, of root, of the life-smell that permeates the Groves. Some wombats find the absence refreshing. Others find it terrifying—the olfactory equivalent of the Crystal Plains' visual sterility.

**Sight:** White to the horizon. The Nico Star's reflection, blinding at noon, golden at dawn and dusk. The sky's curve visible at the edges of vision, a subtle reminder that this flatness is an illusion of scale. The Dingoes' stations as dark spots on the white—dark by contrast, not actually dark—visual relief in an expanse of sameness.

---

## Cultural Resonance

The Salt Pan is the edge of Melborow's influence. The Council claims it, on maps, but does not patrol it. The Tally does not function here. The Frequency reaches the perimeter but loses coherence in the salt's conductivity.

**For the Dingoes:** Home, of a kind. Neutral ground. The place where their packs can meet without territorial conflict, where their logic functions best, where the world's noise is reduced to manageable levels.

**For the wombats:** A place to go when the Council cannot be endured. The Lone Borrows are reachable via the Pan's western edge. The Groves via the eastern. The Pan itself is traverseable only with Dingo guidance—or with the particular desperation that makes guidance unnecessary.

**For the Kangaroos:** A boundary. They do not enter the Pan. Their mobs control the Burnt Scrub to the east, the Dry Channels to the north. The Pan is too flat for their patrol tactics, too open for their comfort.

---

## Drip Elements

**Fragments:** The Dingo signal-patterns—cracks in the salt arranged in configurations that carry meaning. The station designs, which follow geometries that predate the Council. The specific trade goods left at specific piles, their meanings legible only to those who know the system.

**Contested Claims:**
- The Council: "The Salt Pan is Crown territory, held in reserve for future development."
- The Dingoes: "The Pan conducts our data. We mapped its conductivity. We built its stations. It is ours by use."
- The Lone Borrows: "The Pan is the path between. Nobody owns the path. Everybody owns the path."

**What the reader feels:** The edge of the world. The neutrality of mineral. The strange fairness of Dingo logic. The flatness as both freedom and exposure.
